## Web3 Direction

For me, the Web3 direction does not start with a token or an abstract crypto idea. It starts with a simpler and more important observation: people, users, clients, participants, developers, creators, partners, and communities are already forming around my projects.

This is a huge force. If we find an effective way to connect these people with each other, give them clear access to products, allow them to participate, receive value, contribute, and become part of one shared system, then a new internal layer appears on top of ordinary services. This is what I see as the real heart of Web3.

Right now, we are starting with practical infrastructure for online business: shops, payments, Telegram Wallet, partner links, referral mechanics, CPA mechanics, payout automation, and access to digital products. This can already be used today. FabricBot allows people to launch sales and accept payments in Telegram, which gives businesses and creators a practical tool — not just a future concept.

But later, this logic can expand. Everything we build can potentially be connected and tokenized: digital products, access, community membership, service users, participant contributions, partner mechanics, educational materials, AI agents, research reports, and internal tools.

For example, several digital products, community membership, and access to services can be combined into one unified system. A person can get access to more products and opportunities at a lower cost than buying everything separately. Inside the ecosystem, this can create a stronger connection between users, products, creators, and partners.

In this sense, Web3 for me is not a separate “crypto add-on.” It is a way to organize access, participation, contribution, and value distribution inside the ecosystem in a new way.

Key ideas of this direction:

1. **People as the core of the ecosystem** — users, clients, community members, partners, and developers become not just an audience, but part of the system.
2. **Unified access to products** — different services, materials, agents, clubs, and tools can be combined into more valuable access packages and levels.
3. **Tokenization of value** — in the future, not only access can be tokenized, but also contribution, participation, partner mechanics, digital products, and internal assets.
4. **New community models** — a community can become not just a chat, but an economic and product layer around the ecosystem.
5. **Partner economy** — users and partners can help distribute products, bring clients, participate in growth, and receive clear rewards for that.
6. **Cheaper and more valuable for the user** — a person gets access to more products, services, and opportunities through one system instead of buying everything separately.

We are still refining the mission and looking for the right form for this direction. But the important point is that this is not only a concept for the future. There is already a product base that can be used today: FabricBot as infrastructure for Telegram sales, payments, Telegram Wallet, partner mechanics, and online business.

The next step is to connect this with community, digital products, AI tools, want2view, AtlasRepo, and other parts of the ecosystem in order to build a unified layer of access, participation, and value.

# 8. Content Distribution Logic for the Agent

This section is an instruction for the future auto-posting system.

## 8.1 General Principle

When content is ready, the system should distribute it across platforms based on quality, priority, and the limits of each platform.

The best materials go to the main channels. Second-level materials go to additional channels. Remaining content goes to archive or overflow channels so it does not disappear.

## 8.2 Priority for Shorts / Vertical Videos

**First level:** YouTube Main — only the top 1–2 Shorts per day.

**Second level:** YouTube Shorts Channel — up to 3 Shorts per day.

**Third level:** Instagram and TikTok — 2 best videos per day if they fit visually and conceptually.

**Fourth level:** X — sometimes 1 vertical video per day if it contains a strong thesis, data, demo, or product insight.

**Fifth level:** YouTube Old / overflow channel — remaining Shorts that should not be thrown away.

## 8.3 If There Is More Content Than the Limits Allow

If there is already enough content for the main channel on a given day, the agent must not overload the channel. It should:

1. Place the best videos into the nearest available slots.
2. Distribute second-level videos to additional channels.
3. Move some strong content to the following days.
4. Send remaining content to the overflow channel.
5. Avoid publishing too much on one channel, even if there is a lot of content.

## 8.4 If There Is Enough Content for Several Days

If there is already enough content for 2–3 days ahead, the agent should schedule publications for the following days instead of publishing everything at once.

The goal is not to “dump all content,” but to create regularity, preserve the quality of the main channels, and use every piece of content as efficiently as possible.

## 8.5 Content Grading

Before publishing, the agent should evaluate every video:

**A-level:** strong video for the main YouTube channel, Instagram, TikTok, and possibly X.

**B-level:** good video for the second-level Shorts channel.

**C-level:** acceptable remaining video for the overflow channel.

**D-level:** weak video that should not be published or should be sent for manual review.

## 8.6 What the Agent Must Consider

The agent must consider:

- publishing limits for each platform;
- channel priority;
- video quality;
- video topic;
- audience fit;
- already scheduled content;
- presence of links and CTAs;
- publication language;
- visual suitability of the video;
- not overloading the main channel;
- not mixing the main YouTube channel with the overflow channel.
